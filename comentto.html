Caio Resende

UX Case Study

Comentto

A research management platform outgrew its Bootstrap roots. I spent a year as an external UX consultant rebuilding the experience from the ground up — design system, survey engine, dashboards, and mobile apps across multiple industries.

November 2017 – October 2018 External UX Consultant Multiple Industries

01

The Platform

What Comentto Does

Comentto is a research management platform that collects feedback in real time. The core idea is simple: place physical totems — tablets mounted on kiosks — in locations where customers, patients, employees, or visitors can answer surveys on the spot. The responses flow instantly to a web dashboard where managers see results as they come in.

What makes it interesting is the range. Comentto operates across aerospace, logistics, food production, insurance, and education. A factory floor, an airport lounge, a university cafeteria, and a hospital waiting room all use the same platform but need fundamentally different survey experiences. The challenge isn't building one good survey tool — it's building one that works everywhere.

The Ecosystem

The platform has four distinct surfaces:

The web admin panel is where managers create surveys, configure question logic, assign surveys to devices, manage physical units, and view dashboards and reports. It's the command center.

The mobile survey experience runs on tablets mounted on totems. This is what end users interact with — clean, focused screens optimized for touch, one question at a time. Star ratings, emoji scales, NPS sliders, radio buttons, checkboxes, and open text.

The mobile analytics app gives managers a companion view on their phones: quick access to results, trends, and alerts without opening a laptop.

The design system ties it all together. Color, typography, spacing, and component patterns shared across every surface.

The four surfaces: physical totem tablet, web admin login, and mobile analytics companion app.

My Role

I joined Comentto in November 2017 as an external UX consultant. The team was small: myself as UX/UI designer, an engineering lead, a business requirements analyst, and the business director. I was responsible for the entire design surface — audit, design system, interaction patterns, and visual design across web and mobile.

The engagement ran in three phases. Phase 1 (two months): a full UX audit, design system outline, and core interface designs. Phase 2 (one month): redesigning the survey creation and experience. Phase 3 (one month, running concurrently with Phase 2): dashboards and the mobile analytics app.

02

The Audit

Outgrowing Bootstrap

When I first opened the existing Comentto platform, the problem was immediately visible. The product had been built on Bootstrap — which made sense for shipping fast in the early days. But Bootstrap's generic components were now actively working against the product. Every page looked like every other Bootstrap app on the internet. There was no visual identity, no design language, no consistency between the web panel and the mobile experience.

The deeper issue was structural. As the engineering team added features, each new page introduced its own layout patterns. Buttons changed size and color depending on who built the screen. Forms used different spacing. Navigation was inconsistent between sections. The platform was functional, but it had no design foundation to scale on.

The Heuristic Analysis

I ran a full heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's usability principles. The findings fell into clear categories: visibility of system status was weak (users couldn't tell if surveys were active or paused), there was no match between system language and user expectations (technical labels everywhere), consistency was poor across screens, and error prevention was nearly absent — users could break survey logic without any warnings.

The audit gave us a prioritized backlog. Instead of redesigning everything at once, we could sequence the work: start with the design system foundation, then rebuild individual surfaces on top of it.

The full heuristic analysis: annotated screenshots evaluating the existing platform against Nielsen's usability principles.

03

Building the Design System

Atomic Design

The solution to Bootstrap's limitations was a custom design system built on atomic design principles. Atoms (colors, typography, icons, spacing tokens), molecules (form fields, buttons, badges), organisms (navigation bars, data tables, card layouts), and templates that compose them into full page patterns.

This wasn't just a component library. It was a shared language between design and engineering. Every component had documented states, spacing rules, and responsive behavior. When the engineering team built a new page, they assembled it from existing parts instead of inventing new ones. That consistency is what Bootstrap was supposed to provide but couldn't, because Bootstrap's components aren't designed for your product — they're designed for everyone's.

The Color System

Comentto's brand centers on teal and mint, which I carried through as the primary action color. But the most important color decision was the sentiment system: green for positive, yellow for neutral, red for negative. This traffic-light pattern runs through every survey result, every dashboard widget, every report chart. It's intuitive because it maps to a mental model everyone already has.

I defined a full palette with primary, secondary, accent, semantic, and neutral scales. Each color has documented contrast ratios and usage rules. The neutral scale handles backgrounds, borders, and body text across both light surfaces (web admin) and dark surfaces (mobile survey screens on totems).

Typography & Components

I moved from Bootstrap's default stack to a clean sans-serif that felt professional without being cold. Type scales, weight rules, and line-height tokens ensure consistency across web and mobile. Every heading level, body size, caption, and label has a defined purpose.

The component library covers form elements (inputs, selects, toggles, radio buttons, checkboxes), feedback elements (toasts, alerts, progress bars), navigation (sidebar, top bar, tabs, breadcrumbs), and data display (tables, charts, stat cards, status badges). Each component is documented with its variants, states, and responsive behavior.

The design system: sentiment color system, palette guidelines, typography scale, form components, button variants, and icon library.

Navigation, data display, feedback elements, card layouts, selection controls, and status badges — all documented with states and responsive rules.

Spacing tokens, breakpoints, data visualization, modals, sidebar navigation, component inventory, and page template compositions.

04

Redesigning the Survey Builder

The Admin Panel

Phase 2 focused on the survey creation experience. This is where managers spend most of their time: building questions, configuring answer types, setting up logic jumps, and previewing how surveys will look on the totem devices.

The old survey builder was a single long form. No visual preview, no drag-and-drop, no way to see the flow of your survey before publishing it. Creating a survey felt like filling out a spreadsheet. I redesigned it as a two-panel layout: the question list on the left, the question editor on the right. You always see the structure of your survey while editing any individual question.

Question Types

Comentto supports a wide range of question types: multiple choice, single choice, open text, star ratings, emoji scales, NPS scores, numeric sliders, date pickers, and image-based options. Each type has its own configuration panel with type-specific settings. Star ratings let you customize the scale (1–5 or 1–10). NPS includes the standard promoter/passive/detractor labeling. Emoji scales map expressions to numeric values for analysis.

I designed each question type to have a consistent configuration pattern: the question text at the top, the answer options in the middle, and type-specific settings at the bottom. This consistency means managers learn the builder once and apply it to any question type without relearning the interface.

The redesigned survey builder: survey list overview, question creation panel, and type-specific configurations for multiple choice and rating scales.

The full range of question types: open text, emoji scale, NPS, numeric slider, date picker, and image-based options.

Logic Jumps

This was one of the most requested features. Managers needed surveys that adapt based on answers — if someone rates their experience below 3 stars, skip to a follow-up question asking what went wrong. If they rate 5 stars, jump to a referral question instead.

I designed the logic builder as a visual flow. Each condition reads like a sentence: "If answer to Question 3 is less than 3, go to Question 7." No programming required. The builder validates logic chains in real time and warns about orphaned questions or circular references that would break the survey flow.

The logic builder: conditional jump configuration, visual branching, sentence-style rules, and real-time validation.

Survey Settings

Beyond questions, each survey needs configuration: a start screen with branding, a thank-you screen, sharing settings, and device assignment. I designed these as dedicated settings panels accessible from the survey editor sidebar. The start screen preview shows exactly what respondents will see on the totem before beginning their survey.

Survey configuration: sharing settings, thank you screen, branded start screen, and device assignment.

05

The Survey Experience

Designing for Totems

The survey experience is what end users actually see. It runs on tablets mounted on physical totems in lobbies, factory floors, classrooms, and waiting rooms. The design constraints are unique: users are standing, often in a hurry, interacting with a touch screen that's not their own device. Every interaction needs to be obvious. No learning curve. One question at a time, large touch targets, immediate visual feedback.

I designed the tablet survey with generous whitespace and oversized interactive elements. The progress bar at the top shows how many questions remain. Each question type fills the screen with its answer options, so there's never any scrolling or hidden content. The design adapts between portrait and landscape orientations depending on how the totem is mounted.

The tablet survey experience: welcome screen, question types with oversized touch targets, progress indicators, and the thank you screen.

Mobile Survey Screens

The mobile version serves two purposes: a fallback when totems aren't available (QR code surveys), and a testing mode for managers previewing their surveys on their phones. The mobile screens needed to feel like the same product as the tablet version while adapting to a much smaller viewport.

Mobile survey setup: welcome screen, device connection, survey selection, and ready confirmation.

Rating Types

I designed six distinct rating input types, each optimized for different feedback contexts. Star ratings for general satisfaction. Emoji scales for emotional responses — they work especially well in multilingual environments where a smiling face communicates across every language barrier. NPS (Net Promoter Score) for the standard 0–10 loyalty metric. Sliders for continuous scales. Thumbs up/down for binary sentiment. And numeric ratings for precise feedback.

Each rating type was designed with accessibility in mind: high contrast between selected and unselected states, large hit areas, and clear visual feedback on selection. The emoji scale was particularly challenging — the expressions needed to be universally readable at small sizes without cultural ambiguity.

Six rating types: star ratings, emoji scale, NPS, slider, thumbs up/down, and numeric — each optimized for different feedback contexts.

Selection Inputs

Beyond ratings, surveys need standard selection inputs. Radio buttons for single-choice questions. Checkboxes for multi-select. I designed both with generous spacing between options and clear visual distinction between selected and unselected states. On a totem where people are tapping quickly, the difference between "selected" and "not selected" needs to be unmistakable at arm's length.

Selection inputs: radio buttons, checkboxes, open text fields, and submission confirmation — all designed for quick totem interaction.

06

Managing the Fleet

Device Management

Comentto's physical totems are deployed across multiple locations simultaneously. A single organization might have twenty tablets running different surveys in different buildings. The device management panel gives administrators a fleet view: every device, its assigned survey, its location, and whether it's online or offline. If a totem goes dark in a factory hallway, the admin knows immediately.

I designed the device list as a data table with inline status indicators. Green for active, gray for offline, amber for errors. Each row expands to show the device's full configuration: its name, assigned survey, unit (physical location), and connection history. Bulk actions let administrators reassign surveys across multiple devices at once — critical when rolling out a new survey across an entire campus.

Device fleet management: overview table with status indicators, device details, configuration, and bulk actions.

Unit Management

Units represent physical locations — a specific floor, building, or department. Each unit can have multiple devices assigned to it. The unit management panel lets administrators organize their physical infrastructure into a logical hierarchy: company → location → unit → device.

I designed the unit view to show device density per location, helping administrators spot gaps in coverage. If a location has high foot traffic but only one totem, the data makes the case for adding more devices. Each unit card shows its active surveys, response count, and device health at a glance.

Unit management: location hierarchy, device assignments, coverage view, and unit-level response metrics.

07

Dashboards & Reports

Real-Time Monitoring

Phase 3 focused on how managers consume the data their surveys collect. The dashboard is the first thing administrators see when they log in. It needed to answer three questions instantly: How are things going right now? What's trending over time? Where should I pay attention?

I designed the dashboard around the traffic-light sentiment system. The top-level view shows overall sentiment distribution — green, yellow, red — before any individual numbers. Pattern recognition is faster than reading. A manager glancing at a dashboard that's mostly green knows things are fine. A flash of red in one location demands immediate attention.

Below the sentiment overview, survey-specific cards show response counts, completion rates, average ratings, and trend arrows. Each card links to a detailed report view. The layout uses a masonry grid that adapts to the number of active surveys — one survey gets the full width, four surveys tile into a 2×2 grid.

The monitoring dashboard: sentiment overview, survey detail cards, trend visualization, and multi-survey comparison grid.

Detailed Reports

Reports go deeper than dashboards. Each survey generates a detailed breakdown: per-question results, sentiment distribution over custom date ranges, response time patterns (when do people answer — morning, lunch, evening?), and cross-tabulation between questions.

I designed the report view as a scrollable single page with collapsible sections. The full report is always available, but managers can focus on specific sections by collapsing the rest. Charts use the same sentiment colors from the design system, so the visual language stays consistent from dashboard to detail. Bar charts show distribution, line charts show trends, and donut charts show proportions — each chosen for the type of data it represents.

Report views: per-question breakdowns, sentiment over time, response patterns, cross-tabulation, category charts, and the full scrollable report.

08

Analytics on the Go

The Mobile Companion

Not every manager sits at a desk. Factory supervisors walk the floor. Hotel managers move between lobbies. They need to check survey results from their phones. The mobile analytics app is the dashboard in your pocket — same data, same sentiment colors, optimized for a smaller screen and one-handed use.

I wireframed the mobile analytics app to prioritize the most common actions: checking today's results, comparing locations, and spotting negative trends. The home screen is a single scrollable feed of active surveys sorted by urgency — surveys with declining sentiment float to the top. Each card shows the survey name, location, response count, and a mini sentiment bar.

Tapping a survey card opens the detail view with the same per-question breakdowns available in the web reports, adapted for mobile. Charts are simplified: fewer axis labels, larger touch targets on interactive elements, and swipe gestures to move between time periods instead of dropdown selectors.

Mobile analytics wireframes: login, survey feed, detail view, per-question breakdown, trend charts, location comparison, alerts, and settings.

09

What I'd Do Differently

A year of work across four surfaces, one design system, and multiple industries. Here's what I learned and what I'd change.

P0 · Critical

User Testing with Real Totem Users

I designed the mobile survey experience based on heuristics and best practices, but I never observed real people interacting with the totems in their actual environments. A factory worker in gloves tapping a screen has different needs than an office visitor browsing a lobby kiosk. In-context user testing at deployment sites would have caught friction I couldn't predict from a desk.

P1 · High

Design System Adoption Metrics

I built the design system and documented it thoroughly, but I didn't set up tracking for adoption. Which components were the engineering team actually using? Which ones were they building custom alternatives to? Without that data, I couldn't iterate on the system based on real usage.

P2 · Medium

Accessibility Beyond Contrast

The sentiment system relies heavily on green/yellow/red, which is problematic for color-blind users. I specified contrast ratios but didn't add pattern or shape alternatives for the traffic-light system. The design system should include iconographic fallbacks alongside color.

P2 · Medium

Offline Resilience for Totems

Totems in factories and remote locations can lose connectivity. The survey experience should queue responses locally and sync when connection returns. I designed the happy path but didn't fully specify the offline degradation experience — what does the totem show when it can't reach the server?

Looking back, the biggest win was the design system. Before it existed, every new feature introduced visual debt. After it shipped, the engineering team could build new pages that looked and felt consistent without design review on every screen. The system became the designer when I wasn't in the room.

Comentto went from a Bootstrap app that looked like everything else to a platform with its own visual identity, a scalable component library, and a survey experience designed for the unique constraints of physical totem deployment. The atomic design foundation means the product can grow without growing messy.
