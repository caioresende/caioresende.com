Caio Resende

UX Case Study

Portals

Turning an agency-style esports operation into a scalable product, reducing time to market by 4x and making Battlefy profitable

Product Designer · 2019–2020 Esports · B2B · Design Systems Adobe XD · Miro · Qualtrics

01

The Platform

What Battlefy Does

Battlefy is an esports tournament platform that powers competitive gaming experiences for millions of players worldwide. The platform connects three key audiences: players looking to compete, brands wanting to activate in esports, and game developers running official competitive programs.

Battlefy's B2B arm worked with some of the biggest names in gaming and entertainment — Red Bull, Riot Games, EA, Nintendo, Zotac, Manchester City, BT, and many more. For each of these clients, Battlefy created branded landing pages called "Portals" — custom-built web experiences where players could discover tournaments, register, and compete under the client's brand.

My Role

I was the Product Designer leading the productization of Portals. This was the first project to test and scale the design function at Battlefy. The cross-functional team included a PM, an engineer, and sales — with Design leading the productization effort. My work spanned UX research, stakeholder interviews, process redesign, information architecture, wireframing, component design, high-fidelity mockups, prototyping, and user testing across dozens of brand implementations.

Two Types of Users

Players — the end users who visited portals to find tournaments, register teams, and compete. They needed clear paths to action, intuitive flows, and experiences that felt like they belonged to the brand they were engaging with.

Clients (Brands) — the B2B customers paying Battlefy to create and run their esports programs. They needed their brand identity reflected accurately, their audiences served effectively, and their campaigns delivered on time.

Portals across major brands — each fully customized with unique branding, layout, and content. Desktop and mobile versions for EA Madden, Riot Grand Prix, and MSE.

02

The Problem

The Agency Trap

Battlefy operated like a digital agency. Every new client engagement meant building a portal from scratch — custom designs, custom code, custom assets. A Street Fighter V tournament for ELeague looked completely different from a Manchester City Esports Cup, which looked nothing like a PUBG Chicken Dinner Open or a Hearthstone Masters Tour. Every portal was a one-off project with unique requirements, timelines, and engineering effort.

This approach had served Battlefy well when the client roster was small. But as the company grew to serve brands across North America, Europe, Asia, and South America, the cracks became impossible to ignore.

Why It Was Unsustainable

Revenue. Each portal consumed significant design and engineering hours, eating into profit margins on client contracts. The cost of delivery was growing faster than revenue.

Scalability. The team couldn't take on more clients without hiring more people. There was no way to serve a growing pipeline with the same resources.

Maintenance. Once a portal was shipped, any update or fix required going back into custom code. Bug fixes for one portal didn't help any other. The technical debt was compounding.

Constraints

The company operated in a culture of data-driven decisions, but lacked tracking for client satisfaction and user engagement on portals. There was no formal way to measure whether branded experiences were actually better than standard tournament pages. Time was tight, and the engineering team was stretched thin across multiple concurrent client engagements.

The Hypothesis

Looking at the dozens of portals we'd built, a pattern was emerging: despite looking different, clients had remarkably similar goals. Could we productize portals? Could we build a system of reusable, brandable components that met client needs while cutting delivery time dramatically?

Before productization: every portal was designed and shipped with a fully custom approach. Each client got a completely different design, increasing delivery time and resourcing.

03

Discovery & Stakeholder Research

Interviewing the Whole Company

Multiple issues were surfacing during B2B engagements, but the root causes weren't clear. I designed and conducted individual interviews with stakeholders from every team involved in portal delivery: Engineers, Product Managers, Designers, and Sales/Account Managers.

Each interview followed a structured methodology covering: current ownership and responsibilities, inefficiencies, deadline effectiveness, blocking issues, cross-team relationships, process feedback, and what each person wanted to start doing, stop doing, and keep doing.

What Engineers Said

Engineers felt they weren't seeing the design finalized before implementation. They were building things in a rush without validated designs. Comet (the design system) could help, but it wasn't being fully leveraged. They wanted more designers and developers working on the system itself.

What Product Managers Said

PMs were being blocked by clients. Their capacity wasn't matching the volume of engagements. They wanted clearer goals before product execution and earlier involvement of the product team in understanding client needs.

What Designers Said

The design team had portal productization and Comet as ownership areas. The main frustration was the gap between design and engineering validation. Testing and validation needed to happen faster, and Comet needed to be a more unified product.

What Sales & Account Managers Said

Account Managers were frustrated with B2B relationship dynamics during and after reviews. They lacked understanding of certain processes and felt the team could benefit from a more concrete process for QA and client feedback loops.

Examples of the custom portal work that stakeholder interviews revealed was straining the team — each requiring unique process flows across multiple departments.

The Root Cause

After compiling and comparing all interview findings, a clear picture emerged. The initial question had been "how can engagements run faster?" But the real problem was that the team wasn't fully understanding client goals early enough. Not understanding client needs upfront was the root cause of rework, missed deadlines, and team frustration.

The solution wasn't just about building faster — it was about bringing the product team into the early stage of client engagement for clarification and early-stage iterations, then systematizing what we'd learned into reusable patterns.

04

Process Redesign

From Chaos to Swim Lanes

Based on the research findings, I designed a completely new process for how Battlefy executed branded engagements. The redesigned workflow defined four clear team swim lanes, each with specific responsibilities and handoff points:

Sales + Account Managers + Solution Architects — Get new experiences. Responsibilities: set deadlines, get client assets, handle satisfaction, collect feedback, manage special requests.

Design — Design experiences. Responsibilities: kits strategy, design, requirements, testing, updates, documentation, high-fidelity mocks, meet client expectations, user testing and A/B testing, user research.

Frontline Engineers — Execute experiences. Responsibilities: turn kits into experiences, test, update, request kit updates, replan roadmaps, submit updates.

Engine Engineers — Build experiences. Responsibilities: kits implementation, versioning, management, updates, testing, merging external updates, replan roadmaps.

Cross-Functional Cooperation

The new process introduced a continuous feedback loop. Instead of a linear handoff where sales threw requirements over the wall to design, who threw mockups to engineering, the product team was now involved from problem definition through learn-and-improve. Each phase — data analysis, user flows, wireframes, mockups — included cross-team checkpoints.

05

The Solution

Productization Strategy

The strategy had three pillars: validate that current client goals could fit a productized model, compile the most-used components and ensure they were reusable, and create different tiers that would serve different client needs.

After analyzing every portal we'd built, I identified that despite wildly different visual styles, the underlying goals and components were remarkably similar. Clients wanted hero banners, tournament listings, onboarding steps, prize information, FAQ sections, social integration, and streaming embeds. The difference was mostly branding and the depth of engagement features.

Three Tiers of Portals

Tier 1 — Player Acquisition. The entry-level portal. Includes client branding, tournament information, prizing, game listings, social links, and FAQ. Built for clients who need a branded presence to attract players to their competitive programs.

Tier 2 — Player Diversity. Everything in Tier 1, plus multi-region support and localization. For clients running programs across multiple geographies who need their portals to adapt to different audiences and languages.

Tier 3 — Player Engagement. The full experience. Tiers 1 and 2 plus loyalty/reward programs, leaderboards, social media hubs, streaming integration, and hospitality features. For the biggest brands who wanted deep, ongoing engagement with their competitive communities.

Wireframes for the core portal components: page structure, hero banner, onboarding steps, game selector, tournament listings, and awards section.

Mapping User Emotions

Beyond functional requirements, each component was designed with primary goals and expected emotions to evoke on users. The journey was mapped through emotional stages — from Aware to Included, Committed, Welcomed, and Motivated. This user-centric approach informed where to place feedback touchpoints and how to measure whether portals were actually delivering better experiences than standard tournament pages.

To collect this data at scale, I proposed integrating Qualtrics through their public API. This allowed us to create non-intrusive feedback components — always-visible feedback, end-of-flow feedback, and contextualized feedback — without interrupting the player's flow. The same approach that had already proven successful with our organizer ratings feature, where we took player feedback from 0.5% to 16%.

06

Design System & Components

Comet: Battlefy's Design System

The productization effort was anchored in Comet — Battlefy's design system that I helped build and evolve. Comet provided the reusable building blocks that made portal productization possible. Each component had documented rules, responsive variants, and clear specifications for both desktop and mobile.

The wireframing process established the anatomy of every portal section: navigation patterns, hero banners with configurable content areas, game selectors with tab and dropdown variants, onboarding step components, tournament structure displays, award/prizing sections, streaming integration, hospitality information, FAQ modules, and footers.

Comet design system components: navigation, colors, buttons, map selector, streaming, hospitality, FAQ, footer, and tournament experience — all documented with desktop and mobile variants.

Mobile-First Competitive Experience

A significant part of the design system focused on the mobile competitive experience. Many esports players, especially in mobile titles like Clash Royale, interacted with tournaments entirely from their phones. The mobile wireframes covered the complete player journey: login, onboarding, tournament discovery, check-in, match playing, score reporting, deck/team selection, notifications, and victory/defeat moments.

Complete mobile competitive experience: from login and onboarding through tournament check-in, match playing, score reporting, and victory/defeat moments.

Client Asset Guide

To streamline delivery even further, I created a complete guide for clients on how to provide proper assets and styling for their portals. This guide standardized what we needed from each brand — style guides, logos, color palettes, hero imagery — so the team could apply client branding to the productized components quickly and consistently, without the back-and-forth that had slowed down every engagement before.

07

High-Fidelity Across Brands

One System, Many Identities

The true test of the productized system was whether the same components could serve wildly different brands while making each portal feel uniquely theirs. The answer was visible in the results: from the dark, high-energy aesthetic of EA Madden to the clean, corporate feel of MSE Esports to the vibrant colors of Riot's college programs — the same underlying structure adapted seamlessly.

Portals were customized based on a style guide and assets provided by the client. The Comet design system's theming layer handled typography, colors, imagery, and spacing — while the component architecture stayed consistent underneath.

High-fidelity mockups across brands: EA Madden, MSE, Riot Grand Prix, Riot Influencers, B&O, and MLB The Show — all built on the same component system with unique brand theming.

Registration Flows Powered by Comet

The RCLS (Riot Collegiate League Series) registration flow demonstrated the design system's maturity. A complex multi-step process — choose action, create team, link accounts, invite players — was built entirely from Comet components. The same flow could be adapted for any client's registration needs by swapping branding and configuring the steps.

RCLS registration flow built entirely with Comet components — a multi-step team creation and joining process that could be reused across any client engagement.

Responsive Across Devices

Every portal mockup was designed for both desktop and mobile from the start. The component system ensured responsive behavior was built into the foundation, not bolted on afterward.

Mobile-responsive versions of branded portals across EA Madden, Riot Grand Prix, Riot Influencers, B&O, MLB, and MSE.

08

Results & Impact

The productization of Portals transformed Battlefy's business model and proved that the design function could drive company-wide impact.

4x Faster Time to Market

Portal delivery went from weeks of custom work to a fraction of the time using the productized component system and tiered approach.

32x Player Feedback Increase

Player ratings on tournaments went from 0.5% to 16% participation through iterative UX improvements to the feedback flow.

62%→80% Player Love Score

Player satisfaction on tournament experiences increased from 62% to 80%, validating that productized portals could match custom quality.

Profitable Business Impact

The reduced cost of portal delivery combined with maintained client satisfaction made Battlefy's B2B operation profitable.

Product Validation

After collecting quantitative and qualitative feedback through the Qualtrics integration, it was time to compare branded portals with non-branded tournament experiences. The data confirmed that branded engagements delivered measurably better player experiences — validating the entire Portals product as a core part of Battlefy's value proposition, not just a services add-on.

09

Scaling the Design Function

From One Project to Many

Portals was the first project to test and scale the design function at Battlefy. Once the productization was validated and the Comet design system matured, other projects could leverage the same foundation — dramatically accelerating their delivery timelines.

CLOL — College League of Legends

The CLOL portal was one of the most complex implementations built on the Comet system. It included league registration flows, player guides, team management, admin tools, standings, fantasy leagues, and player profiles. The design system handled this complexity because the components were already battle-tested through Portals — what would have been months of custom work was delivered efficiently on the existing foundation.

CLOL (College League of Legends) — a full league management system built on Comet: portal pages, fantasy leagues, player profiles, admin tools, and team management.

Apex Legends Competitive

The Apex Legends implementation showcased the system's flexibility for team-based battle royale formats. The Compete tab handled complex states — not joined, joined, team member views, check-in, lobby management, and multi-round tournament progression — all through configurable Comet components. The team captain lineup management and tournament card states were new patterns that extended the design system's vocabulary.

Apex Legends competitive experience: compete tab states, team lineups, captain management, and the navigation style guide — all extending the Comet design system.

The Bigger Picture

The Portals productization wasn't just about building faster landing pages. It established that design could drive strategic, company-wide transformation at Battlefy. The design system, the process redesign, the tiered product strategy, and the data-driven validation framework all became foundational to how the company operated going forward. What started as a project to speed up portal delivery ended up proving that investing in design infrastructure could fundamentally change a company's economics.
