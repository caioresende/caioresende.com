01

The Problem Nobody Was Solving

When I started my Canadian citizenship process, the first thing I did was look for an app. I found fifteen of them. Every single one was a quiz app. Study for the test, take practice questions, check your score. That's it.

But the citizenship process isn't just a test. It's years (!) of tracking physical presence days, organizing stacks of documents across eight categories, and studying content that spans Canadian history, geography, rights, and government. The test is the last step, not the whole journey.

No app treated it that way. Some had decent question banks, but they were all missing the same things: presence tracking (the legal requirement to prove you've lived in Canada long enough), document management (the paperwork that actually makes or breaks your application), and any sense of progress beyond "you scored 70%."

I wanted an app that sat with me through the whole process. Not just test prep. A companion for the full citizenship journey. I also wanted to give people technology to be confident about their application, instead of paying thousands of dollars for an immigration lawyer.

Some examples from my competitive analysis of 15 citizenship apps. Every single one focuses on quizzes. None offer presence tracking or document management.

02

The Spark

The Architecture

I chose a hub-and-spoke information architecture. The home screen is the hub, and each major feature (quiz, presence, documents) is a spoke you reach from there. For an app with only a handful of primary destinations, this keeps navigation flat and direct. Every major action is one tap away from home, no hamburger menus, no hidden drawers.

The tech stack was Flutter for cross-platform, Firebase for the backend, RevenueCat for monetization, and Cloud Functions for the heavy lifting like presence day calculations and quiz stats processing. I needed a stack that could move fast and handle real-time data without me spending weeks on infrastructure.

Pen and paper, and ideas...

I started sketching on mid January. I still like to dump ideas on pen and paper. The freedom to just start again and not polish it all is freeing. Because I went through the process myself, I knew I had to just sketch features that went beyond a quiz app.

But the quiz app was the starting point. I mapped out a report system: knowledge gap heatmaps, a readiness score, learning curves over time, average time per question. I also knew I had to create features that would make my app stands out from the other quiz apps. I wanted to help people understand how they learn, not just what they got wrong.

A couple of days later, I had a sprint plan with numbered priorities. I wanted a strong foundation, so that I was motivated to build a complete app. Upload test questions to Firebase. Create cloud functions to process data. Store history for user based on questions answered. The timeline was tight: six weeks from paper to product. Spoiler alert, It worked!

The Data Model

Each question carries more than just a prompt and four options. Product design goes beyond UI. If you're a solo builder, you have to stop and think about eveything. Having a strong backgorund in coding, allowed me to think about the data structure, so the UX/UI would take advantage of it all.

I sketched the full data structure early: number, ID, category, prompt, options A through D, difficulty level, a full explanation shown after answering, and a reference to the page in the official study guide. On the stats side: attempts, correct attempts, whether they got it right on the first try, if they asked for an explanation, each option users picked, skipped count, time to answer, and the confidence score.

That level of tracking is what makes the reporting meaningful. Most quiz apps record right or wrong. This one records how you answered, how fast, how confident, and whether you bothered reading the explanation.

Confidence Matrix

This is the feature I'm most proud of. Users answer the question with a tap, combined with a slider asking how confident they were. Tracking Presence

Knowing how long you've been in Canada
is crucial. Counting down the days for the citizenship is seamless.

This is the feature that no competitor has. To apply for Canadian citizenship, you need to prove you've been physically present in Canada for at least 1,095 days within the five years before your application. That's a legal requirement, and miscounting can delay or reject your application.

Most people track this in a spreadsheet or not at all. I wanted the app to handle it automatically.

I sketched the presence calculator on paper first. The core logic runs as a Cloud Function: it takes your arrival date, calculates how many days you've been physically present, accounts for absences you log, and tells you exactly where you stand. The onboarding screen collects the essentials: date of arrival, immigration status, and first entry date.

Paper sketches for the presence tracker: cloud function architecture, calendar entry system, and dashboard data structure.

UX analysis of the government tool

Before designing my own solution, I needed to understand what already existed. The Government of Canada offers a Physical Presence Calculator on their website — the only official tool available for applicants to check if they meet the 1,095-day requirement. I performed a UX audit of this tool to identify pain points, usability issues, and opportunities that my app could address.

The analysis revealed 10 significant usability issues ranging from poor visual hierarchy and information overload to confusing date formats and unnecessary mandatory fields. These findings directly shaped the design decisions for the presence tracker in Canadian Tomorrow — every issue I found became a design constraint I committed to solving.

1. Visual hierarchy and layout. There's a lack of visual hierarchy, which makes it hard to know where to start. Headers could be larger, and important sections could use color or other styling to stand out. Spacing between sections is minimal, making it feel cramped.

2. Dense Text and Information Overload. Landing page contains long blocks of text without much separation or visual hierarchy, which can be overwhelming and hard to scan. Important information, like the "1,095-day requirement", is buried in the text.

3. Mobile Responsiveness. Although the design is responsive, there are some flaws to its implementation, where excessive text, lack of visual hierarchy and a better content strategy. Content density is too high.

4. Sequence of flow is confusing. Although the page explains what data users need to enter, it could benefit from visual examples or tooltips next to each instruction. Having form fields directly on the page with dynamic instructions could make it more interactive.

5. UI could be more polished. The current state of the UI presents many areas for improvements including credibility, accessibility, consistency, clarity and focus. In many moments users are overwhelmed with unpolished components and lack of aesthetic appeal.

6. Excessive amount of text. Government tools tend to be excessive on how they present information and instructions, potentially slowing down task completion. The use of images and icons can reduce the amount of text and improve scan-ability overall.

7. Dates are not user friendly. Dates are displayed in a format that is hard to read at first, making users connect numbers to a specific month. This becomes specially more complex when immigrants are not used to how they learned how to write and read date formats.

8. Date picker prone to user error. There is a change to avoid user error by limiting date range to specific time window. Since the government expects your calculation to happen in the past 5 years, the system fails to limit that, allowing dates to be picked outside of the range.

9. Unnecessary mandatory fields. The system requires users to add information that is not important to them at the moment of calculating a preview of their citizenship journey. For example, when you're absent, the system expects a reason – this is not relevant before actually applying.

10. Confusing CTAs. CTAs are crucial to guide and keep users in the flow. The system uses generic CTAs in all screens, for example "Calculate" is used many steps before actually calculating your physical presence. Something like "Continue" or "Proceed to [phase name]" is more appropriate.

Onboarding Presence

Before the app can calculate anything, it needs to know when you arrived. The onboarding flow collects three key data points: your date of entry into Canada, your immigration status at the time, and whether you held temporary resident status before becoming a permanent resident. Getting this right on the first try was critical — if users enter incorrect dates during onboarding, every calculation downstream becomes unreliable.

I designed the onboarding as a focused, step-by-step flow rather than a single long form. Each screen asks one question with clear context about why it matters. The status selector uses plain language instead of legal jargon, and a progress indicator shows users exactly where they are in the setup. The goal was to make a legally complex process feel approachable — three screens, under two minutes, and you're tracking.

Entry pattern. Some exploration and evolution of how the pattern for an date entry was designed. Every period has a start and end date plus a type. It’s used on onboarding and entry modals.

Customization.  Asking the right questions early on helps the users understand their tasks and statuses. Specially for the presence feature, knowing if they are temporary or permanent. Date picker is clean, isolated because it’s crucial to get them correct.

Date picker. I designed two ways of handling dates: if users are adding, it’s a date picker range, but if editing, a single one. It aligns better with the action of setting and tuning it.

Adding and editing entries

Once onboarded, users need to log every absence from Canada. Each entry requires a departure date, return date, and destination. The same fields the government asks for on the official application. I designed the entry form to validate dates in real time: it flags overlapping absences, warns if a trip falls outside your eligibility window, and auto-calculates the number of days abroad so users never have to count manually.

Editing existing entries had to be just as frictionless. Users can tap any row in their absence list to modify it, with the form pre-filled and changes reflected instantly in the day count. There is a little calendar animation that not only elevate the experience, but makes the user pay attention to the new date. The principle was simple: if the government makes you track it, the app should make it painless to maintain.

Entry type. Initially all types were together. After knowing the status transition, temporary and permanent types were separated for more transparency and less errors from users.

Confirmation nudge. Every entry has a duration. I added a feedback counting the days on every interaction for support.

The dashboard shows your total physical presence days front and center, broken down by permanent resident days, temporary resident days (counted at half credit), and days remaining to reach the 1,095-day threshold. A calendar animates your progress visually so you can see at a glance where you stand.

Below the summary, a calendar-style absence log lists every trip with dates, destination, and duration. I added a projected eligibility date that updates dynamically — if you add a new absence, the date shifts immediately.

The dashboard transforms a spreadsheet exercise into a living, breathing status page that tells you not just where you are, but exactly when you'll be ready to apply.

Updating profile

Immigration status changes. People transition from temporary workers to permanent residents, update their entry dates after finding old travel records, or correct mistakes they made during onboarding. The profile section lets users update their immigration status, key dates, and personal details without starting over. Every change triggers a recalculation of the presence dashboard, so the impact of a correction is visible immediately.

I designed the profile editor with safeguards: changing your status type shows a confirmation explaining how it affects your day count (temporary days count as half), and date changes display a before-and-after comparison of your total presence.

The goal was to make corrections feel safe rather than scary, no one should avoid fixing a mistake because they're afraid of breaking their calculation or progress so far.

Organizing Documents

The citizenship application requires documents across eight categories: application form, photos, identity documents, language proficiency, physical presence proof, tax records, citizenship photos, and test readiness. Each category has its own rules for what format is accepted: paper originals, color copies, scanned digital files, or online forms.

I started with a sketch dated February 23. The structure was clear from the beginning: folders containing mandatory documents, the ability to upload and mark files, and the system showing validation status for each folder once the required files are ready. I built a table mapping each document type to its paper and online requirements.

Document sketches covering folder structure, file states, progress tracking, and design references from iOS file management apps.

For design inspiration, I studied how the best file management apps handle similar problems. iOS Files, Apple Notes, and various folder-based UIs informed my approach. The key insight was that document management for citizenship isn't really about files. It's about progress. You need to see at a glance which categories are complete, which are missing items, and what you need to do next.

The documents dashboard went through seven iterations. Each one refined the balance between the folder list, progress indicators, and individual document status. I tested different icon styles, progress badge placements, and color treatments for complete versus incomplete states. The file management screens handle the actual upload and tracking: document type, status (pending, approved, rejected), language, and a preview area.

Making It Sustainable

I studied how other citizenship and learning apps monetize. The landscape is mostly subscriptions: monthly or annual fees for "premium" access. But for a citizenship app, subscriptions don't make sense. The process has a clear end date. You study, you pass, you're done. Asking someone to pay monthly for something they'll use for three to six months felt wrong.

I went with one-time pricing instead. Three tiers that unlock features permanently: no recurring charges, no "your access expires" anxiety. For something as stressful as citizenship, the last thing people need is subscription pressure on top of everything else.

Paper sketches covering landing page structure, pricing tiers, paywall triggers, feature gating, and tier comparison.

I designed feature cards that explain exactly what each tier unlocks: all 400 questions, quiz customization, confidence-based analytics, document analysis, focused stats, study mode, and unlimited test simulations. Each card shows a screenshot of the actual feature so users can see what they're paying for.

The paywall appears contextually. When you tap a locked feature, you see a paywall specific to that feature rather than a generic "upgrade" screen. The headline stays the same across contexts, but the featured card at the top changes to match what you were trying to access. This respects the user's intent instead of interrupting them with irrelevant upselling. I studied what other apps do wrong here: generic walls that feel disconnected from what the user wanted. Contextual paywalls convert better because they answer the question you already had.

What I'd Do Differently

I designed this app from my own experience as an immigrant going through the Canadian citizenship process. That gave me strong instincts about what matters, but it also means I have blind spots. Here's what I know needs work.

P0 · Critical

User Testing

I built this based on my journey, but citizenship processes vary by country of origin, immigration status, and province. A Brazilian permanent resident in Ontario has a different experience than a Syrian refugee in Quebec. Real user testing with diverse immigrants would surface problems I can't see from my own perspective.

P1 · High

Accessibility Audit

The confidence slider needs screen reader testing. Sliders are notoriously difficult for assistive technology, and this one is a core interaction. I'd also audit the color-coded quiz categories to ensure they work for color-blind users with pattern or shape alternatives.

P2 · Medium

Dark Mode

The sage and cream palette doesn't have a dark counterpart yet. For an app people might use at night while studying, that's a real gap.

P2 · Medium

Analytics Integration

The confidence slider is my biggest bet, but I don't have data yet to validate whether it actually changes study behavior. I'd want to see: do people study more after seeing they got lucky? Does the confidence matrix drive focused review? Without analytics, the hypothesis stays unproven.

P2 · Medium

Offline Support for Documents

The quiz and presence features work offline through cached data. But document management currently requires a connection for uploads. For people in remote areas or with unstable internet, offline document staging with background sync would make the app more reliable.

If I had another month, the user testing and accessibility audit would come first. The confidence slider hypothesis needs real data. And dark mode would ship alongside it.

Six weeks ago, every citizenship app was just a quiz. Now there's one that tracks your presence days, organizes your documents, helps you understand how you learn, and does it all in four languages across seven device sizes. It's not just test prep anymore. It's a companion for the whole journey.
