Caio Resende

UX Case Study

Valence

Unlocking activation & scalable design through onboarding new users

Lead Product Designer · 2021–2023 SaaS · Enterprise Figma · Heap · FullStory

01

The Platform

What Valence Does

Valence is a team diagnostic platform used by Fortune 500 companies like Coca-Cola, Nestle, and Vanguard. It offers a suite of four tools — Perspective (personality assessments), Align (virtual team facilitator), Reflect 360 (growth-oriented feedback), and Habits (commitment and accountability) — all designed to help leaders understand and improve how their teams work together.

The platform serves enterprise HR and L&D teams who purchase licenses for their organizations. Activation was the lifeblood of the business model: revenue renewal depended directly on how many teams were actually using the tools, not just how many had accounts.

My Role

I joined as the first in-house design lead after multiple cycles of contractors. My mandate was twofold: create a universal design language across all four products, and deliver a consistent, clean experience in the five months leading up to the Series A round.

I led a team of two UX/UI designers, working alongside three user interviewers, a customer success representative, and a UX writer. My role covered discovery, solution design, and testing — from auditing existing flows in FullStory to prototyping and validating with Fortune 500 leaders.

The Valence product suite, the registration experience, and the project timeline spanning discovery through engineering.

02

The Problem

40% of Teams Were Going Dark

The data was stark: 40% of teams created on the platform were never activated. Organizations were buying licenses, setting up accounts, and then nothing. Leaders would register their teams but never run a single exercise. The tools were sitting there, unused.

This wasn't a minor gap. For an enterprise SaaS company approaching Series A, inactive teams meant threatened renewals. Each enterprise contract could be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars — a single client like Coca-Cola represented a $400,000 contract. If teams weren't activating, the entire revenue model was at risk.

The Key User

Team leaders were the critical persona. They were the ones who received the license, set up their teams, and decided whether the tools would be adopted. If a leader didn't activate, their entire team stayed dormant. One disengaged leader could mean five, ten, or twenty inactive members.

The data backed this up: leaders had a 3x higher bounce rate than team members after completing onboarding. The people who mattered most were the ones leaving fastest.

Constraints

We were working under tight conditions. There was no time for thorough, traditional user interviews. The team was limited. And our earlier attempts at interviewing enterprise clients directly hadn't yielded actionable insights — these were busy executives who gave polished answers, not honest ones. We needed a different approach.

The activation gap: 40% of teams going dark, leaders bouncing at 3x the rate of members, and the business impact on enterprise contracts.

03

Discovery

Starting With Hypotheses

Since traditional interviews weren't working, I turned to behavioral data. But before diving into analytics, we wrote down what we believed was happening. Three hypotheses:

1. Users don't understand why the tools are beneficial.
2. They don't know how to get started or where to go first.
3. Our marketing within the product is non-existent — it's simply too hard to make sense of what's on offer.

Mapping the Flows

I mapped the entire user journey from registration through exercise completion: Register → Onboarding → Welcome → Start Exercise → Invite Team → Close Exercise. Then I went granular, diagramming every screen, decision point, and branch in the existing onboarding flow. We annotated the map with FullStory session recordings and Heap analytics, flagging conversion drop-offs and bugs at each step.

From hypotheses to flow mapping: we charted every step of the onboarding journey, annotated with analytics and session recordings.

What the Metrics Told Us

The quantitative findings were damning. 30% of users were bypassing onboarding entirely, jumping straight past the questions that were meant to personalize their experience. Leaders had a 3x bounce rate compared to team members after onboarding. The onboarding itself was the most disconnected part of the entire flow — users arrived on different pages completely lost, with questions that didn't actually personalize anything meaningful.

What Users Showed Us

User testing sessions added the qualitative layer. The onboarding questions were clear enough, but users spent too much time reading without good scannability. After completing onboarding, they landed on a page full of options with no guidance. They changed tabs right away, hunting for something that felt like a starting point. They didn't perceive value on first contact. The first-mile experience was completely disconnected from the promise made during registration.

Metrics and testing told the same story: onboarding was failing to orient users, and the first-mile experience was disconnected.

04

Improving the Onboarding

With clear findings in hand, we defined three areas of improvement:

1. Improve the onboarding UI and revisit the questions.
2. Improve the first page users see after onboarding.
3. Improve product landing pages so leaders understand the benefits before starting.

Scannability and Education

The original onboarding Step 1 asked "Help us understand your situation" with plain radio buttons and a "Does not apply" option. The text was functional but dense. I redesigned it as large, styled radio cards with clear role labels — Team Member, Manager, HRBP/Facilitator — each with a brief description of what to expect. The visual weight shifted from reading paragraphs to scanning cards.

Step 2 followed the same pattern. The original had plain checkboxes listing objectives. The redesign turned each option into a card with contextual preview images — "Team bonding," "Build trust," "Endorse hybrid work" — giving users both a label and a visual anchor. The number of steps was expanded from 3 to 4 to reduce cognitive load per screen.

Before and after: plain radio buttons became styled role cards, and checkboxes became visual objective selectors with preview images.

Prototyping in Figma

Every iteration was prototyped in Figma with interactive flows, not static screens. This let us test the full onboarding sequence with users — clicking through role selection, objective picking, and the landing experience — in a realistic context rather than asking people to imagine what comes next.

Interactive Figma prototypes let us test the complete onboarding flow with real users before engineering.

05

Redesigning Product Pages

Benchmark and Inspiration

Before redesigning the product landing pages, I benchmarked how other tools handled the same challenge — getting new users to understand value and start using the product. I studied Google Calendar's onboarding, Pages by Apple, Officevibe, and Twitter's tools. The common thread: the best first-contact experiences sell the outcome, not the feature.

The First Contact Problem

The existing product pages were sparse. When a leader clicked into Align, they saw a basic card layout with little context. No testimonials, no outcome descriptions, no step-by-step explanation of what would happen. For a tool that asks you to run a team diagnostic, that's too much friction. Users need to understand what they're committing to before they start.

Benchmarking against Google Calendar, Officevibe, and others, then applying those patterns to transform sparse product cards into rich landing experiences.

New Components

I designed a complete set of new components for the product pages. The redesigned Align page opened with a clear headline — "What is Align?" — followed by three illustrated steps: Uncover what matters most (Diagnostic), Receive coach insights (Insights), and Align on the path forward (Discuss). Each step showed users exactly what would happen, removing the uncertainty that was causing leaders to bounce.

Below the steps, I added a "Why managers use Align" section with concise benefit cards, an FAQ addressing the most common hesitations ("Do team members like Align?", "What's the ideal team size?"), customer testimonials from Vanguard leaders, and a persistent CTA to start the exercise. Every section was designed to answer a doubt and move the user closer to starting.

The new product page: step-by-step guide, benefit cards, FAQ, customer testimonials, and persistent CTAs.

Exploring Alternatives

I explored multiple variations of the product landing page before converging on the final design. Different headline treatments, different hero layouts, and different ways of presenting the product preview. I also experimented with motion graphics — side-by-side comparisons of the Align exercise as animated previews — to give users a feel for the interaction before committing.

Multiple landing page explorations, motion graphic comparisons, and the final Align exercise interaction design.

06

Results & Learnings

We shipped the redesigned onboarding and product pages in a phased rollout, monitoring metrics closely through Heap and FullStory.

+10% Conversion Increase

Early results showed a 10% increase in activation conversion within the first two weeks of launch.

↑ GIF Engagement

Users spent more time on product pages engaging with animated previews, validating the motion graphics investment.

Top CTA Engagement

Users weren't scrolling to the bottom but were engaging with CTAs at the top, informing future placement decisions.

8 wks Total Timeline

From discovery through engineering: 2 weeks discovery, 2 weeks iteration, 1 week testing, 1 week iteration, 2 weeks engineering.

Early learnings from the redesign and feature adoption growth trends over 180 days.

Key Takeaways

Behavioral data over interviews. When direct client interviews didn't produce actionable insights, FullStory recordings and Heap analytics became our primary research tools. Watching real users get lost after onboarding was more valuable than any interview transcript.

The first mile is everything. The gap between "registered" and "activated" was entirely a first-mile design problem. Users weren't rejecting the tools — they just couldn't figure out where to start. Fixing the path from onboarding to first exercise was the highest-leverage change we could make.

Show the outcome, not the feature. The original product pages described what each tool did. The redesigned pages showed what would happen when you used them. That shift — from feature description to outcome preview — was the single biggest driver of engagement.

07

What I'd Try Next

If I had more time with this project, here's what I'd focus on.

Personalized Post-Onboarding Routing

The redesigned onboarding collects role and objective data, but the routing after completion was still generic. I'd build truly personalized first-contact pages based on what users tell us during onboarding — a manager focused on team bonding should land on a different page than an HRBP evaluating the platform for multiple teams.

Activation Drip Campaigns

Design can only do so much within the product. I'd partner with marketing to build email sequences triggered by onboarding completion that guide leaders through their first exercise step by step, with links straight to the right product page.

Deeper Motion Integration

The GIF experiments showed clear engagement gains. I'd invest in richer animated previews — short, looping demonstrations of each tool's core interaction — embedded directly in both the product pages and the onboarding flow. Showing the experience is more persuasive than describing it.

Cross-Tool Discovery

Most leaders who activated only used one tool. I'd design cross-promotion moments within the exercise completion flow: "You just ran Align — your team might also benefit from Reflect 360." Turning single-tool users into multi-tool users would significantly improve per-team engagement.
